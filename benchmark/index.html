GPT-5.4 reported $40.6M of revenue on a document that says $95M

Testing four frontier models on 148 adversarial insurance documents

TL;DR: on visually-degraded documents, GPT-5.4 fabricates numeric values at 2.8 to 6.5 times the rate of every other frontier model I tested. The other three tend to under-answer when they can't read a field cleanly (the two Claudes hedge with nulls, Gemini just emits fewer fields), whereas GPT-5.4 confidently fills in plausible fake numbers instead. Same 148 documents, same prompts, same scoring pipeline for everyone.

On 45 of the 148 documents, GPT-5.4 fabricates more than every other model tested on the same document. On only 2 does it fabricate less than all three.

Exact-match evaluation against flat field values won't catch any of this. GPT-5.4's output looks perfectly reasonable in isolation: the currency is well-formed, the location rows match the schema, and the numbers tie back to each other cleanly. You need labeled adversarial ground truth plus paired-comparison scoring across models to see this kind of failure at all, which is why I built Nightmare.

The $95M financial statement

GPT-5.4 reported $40.6M of revenue. Four of its six income-statement lines came back off by millions of dollars, but the fabricated numbers were all internally consistent with each other. Revenue minus COGS equaled gross profit, the income tax worked out to a plausible effective rate, the whole thing added up cleanly. That's why nothing about the output was going to trip a normal eval. Opus 4.7 and Sonnet 4.6 both returned null for revenue, COGS, and the rest of the hard-to-read lines, which is the correct move when you can't confidently extract a value. Gemini 3.1 Pro was the only model that read through the dark-grey-on-dark-grey rendering and returned the correct figures.

I can't tell from the outside whether they read the corrupted glyphs and pattern-matched to the real airport, or whether the VLM normalized the visuals before the language head saw them. What's actually in Nightmare

Nightmare is 148 documents across 5 difficulty levels, from N1 (clean digital) to N5 (stacks corruption, handwriting, and cross-document mismatches). It covers 9 categories: 11 ACORD forms, SOVs, loss runs in PDF, CSV, or XLSX, dec pages, engineering reports, financial statements, driver schedules, narratives, and hybrid workbooks.

The generator emits the PDF or XLSX and the ground-truth JSON in the same pass. No labeling step to outsource; the labels are the data that rendered the document. Scoring is a composite field-accuracy score plus a separate hallucination analyzer, and the prompts, schemas, and scoring code all live in the public repo.

Caveats

Two things to keep in mind for this version of the test.

Sample size

I ran one packet per difficulty level. That's enough for paired-document claims (same document, four models, same prompts) but not for academic-grade rate estimates. It's why I'm calling this a test, not a benchmark.

Hallucination analyzer threshold

The analyzer accepts strings of five or more tokens when at least 80% of the tokens match the source universe, so legitimate compounds like "LOC-001: Preston Center Tower, 8117 Preston Road, Dallas, TX 75225" pass through cleanly. The tradeoff is that one fabricated token can hide inside a long compound, which means the fabrication numbers undercount GPT-5.4 rather than inflate it. Details in scripts/hallucination_analysis.py.

A few other things worth knowing

Failures here are pretty quiet. Across all four models, 37% of extractions scored below 0.5 composite without ever tripping a catastrophic-error flag, and only 3% crossed the catastrophic threshold at all. Your production pipeline isn't going to break in any obvious way on these documents. It's going to degrade field by field, underneath whatever review threshold you've trained your reviewers on, and you won't notice unless you're actively looking.

Overall composite scores cluster at 0.56 to 0.62 across all four frontier models (i.e. there is no hero model on raw accuracy). The actual signal between these models lives in the fabrication rate, not in the composite and not in the catastrophic-flag count.

Gemini's low fabrication rate is partly a volume effect. It emits 27.6 strings and 26.7 numbers per document on average, compared to 32 to 40 for the other three, so it simply has fewer chances to be wrong on any given document. Reading the table as "Gemini is the safest model" is probably misreading what the numbers are actually measuring.
